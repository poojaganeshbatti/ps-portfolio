Statistical Inference

1. Need for Sampling

In many real-life situations, studying the entire population is either impossible or impractical due to limitations such as time, cost, and accessibility. Sampling provides an effective alternative by allowing conclusions about the population to be drawn using a smaller, manageable subset of data.

A properly chosen sample can accurately represent the characteristics of the population. Sampling is widely used in engineering, quality control, social sciences, and data analysis to make reliable decisions with limited resources.

2. Sampling Methods

Sampling techniques determine how units are selected from a population. The choice of method affects the accuracy and reliability of results.

Simple Random Sampling With Replacement (SRSWR):

In SRSWR, each unit selected is returned to the population before the next draw. As a result, the same unit may appear multiple times in the sample. Each draw is independent and has equal probability.

Simple Random Sampling Without Replacement (SRSWOR):

In SRSWOR, once a unit is selected, it is not returned to the population. This ensures that all observations in the sample are distinct and dependent on previous selections.

3. Hypothesis Testing

Hypothesis testing is a formal statistical procedure used to make decisions about population parameters based on sample information. It involves testing an assumption (hypothesis) to determine whether it is supported by the data.

Two hypotheses are formulated: the null hypothesis and the alternative hypothesis. Statistical evidence from the sample is used to decide whether to reject or accept the null hypothesis.

Null and Alternative Hypotheses

The null hypothesis (H₀) represents a statement of no change or no difference. The alternative hypothesis (H₁) represents the presence of a difference or effect.

H₀ : μ = μ₀
H₁ : μ ≠ μ₀ (Two-tailed)
H₁ : μ > μ₀ (Right-tailed)
H₁ : μ < μ₀ (Left-tailed)

4. Z-Test

A Z-test is used for testing hypotheses about population means or proportions when the sample size is large or when the population standard deviation is known. It relies on the standard normal distribution.

Conditions for Using Z-Test

  • Sample size n ≥ 30
  • Population standard deviation σ is known
  • Population is approximately normal
  • Observations are independent

Z-Test Statistic

The Z-statistic measures how far the sample mean deviates from the hypothesized population mean in terms of standard error.

Z = ( x̄ − μ₀ ) / ( σ / √n )

Decision Rule

The calculated Z-value is compared with the critical value obtained from the standard normal distribution table to decide whether to reject the null hypothesis.

  • Two-tailed test: Reject H₀ if |Z| > 1.96
  • Right-tailed test: Reject H₀ if Z > 1.645
  • Left-tailed test: Reject H₀ if Z < −1.645

5. Confidence Intervals

A confidence interval provides a range of values within which the true population parameter is expected to lie with a specified level of confidence. It reflects both accuracy and reliability of estimation.

Confidence Interval = Point Estimate ± Margin of Error

Confidence Interval for Mean (σ Known)

When the population standard deviation is known and the sample size is large, the Z-distribution is used to construct the confidence interval.

x̄ ± zα/2 (σ / √n)

Confidence Interval for Mean (σ Unknown)

If the population standard deviation is unknown and the sample size is small, the t-distribution is used with (n − 1) degrees of freedom.

x̄ ± tα/2 (s / √n)

Confidence Interval for Population Proportion

Confidence intervals for proportions are used to estimate the fraction of the population possessing a certain characteristic.

p̂ ± zα/2 √[ (p̂q̂) / n ]

6. Tests for Mean

Tests for mean help determine whether a sample mean significantly differs from a hypothesized population mean.

Single Population (σ Known)

Z = (x̄ − μ₀) / (σ / √n)

Single Population (σ Unknown)

t = (x̄ − μ₀) / (s / √n)

7. Tests for Difference of Means

These tests are used to compare the means of two independent populations to determine whether a significant difference exists.

Population SD Known

Z = (x̄₁ − x̄₂) / √(σ₁²/n₁ + σ₂²/n₂)

Population SD Unknown (Pooled t-test)

sp² = [(n₁−1)s₁² + (n₂−1)s₂²] / (n₁ + n₂ − 2)
t = (x̄₁ − x̄₂) / [sp √(1/n₁ + 1/n₂)]

8. Tests for Proportion

Proportion tests are used to analyze categorical data and determine whether observed proportions differ significantly from assumed values.

Single Population

Z = (p̂ − p₀) / √(p₀q₀ / n)

Difference of Two Proportions

p = (x₁ + x₂) / (n₁ + n₂)
Z = (p₁ − p₂) / √[pq(1/n₁ + 1/n₂)]
