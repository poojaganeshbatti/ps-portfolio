Chapter 1: Description of Data

1. Types of Variables

In statistics, a variable is any characteristic or attribute that can take different values from one observation to another. Understanding the type of variable is essential because it determines the appropriate statistical methods and graphical representations.

  • Qualitative (Categorical): Qualitative variables describe non-numerical characteristics. They represent qualities or categories rather than quantities.
    • Nominal variables: Categories without any natural order (e.g., gender, blood group, type of device).
    • Ordinal variables: Categories with a meaningful order but unequal intervals (e.g., grades, ranks, satisfaction levels).
  • Quantitative (Numerical):
    • Discrete: Countable values like number of students or calls.
    • Continuous: Measurable values like time spent online or call duration.

2. Scales of Measurement

  • Nominal: Classification only (gender, blood group).
  • Ordinal: Ordered categories (ranks, grades).
  • Interval: Equal intervals, no true zero (temperature).
  • Ratio: True zero exists (height, time).

3. Frequency Distribution

    A frequency distribution organizes data into classes or categories and shows how often each value occurs.

    Relative Frequency Distribution
  • Expresses frequency as a proportion or percentage of the total.
  • Useful for comparing data sets of different sizes.

    Cumulative Frequency Distribution
  • Shows the accumulation of frequencies up to a certain value.
  • Helps in identifying medians, quartiles, and percentiles.

5. Measures of Central Tendency & Dispersion

Measures of central tendency describe the center of data, while dispersion measures the spread.

  • Central Tendency: Mean, Median, Mode
  • Dispersion: Range, Variance, Standard Deviation

6. Situations to Use Mean, Median & Mode

    • Mean: Used for symmetric data without extreme values.
    • For Grouped Data

      • xˉ = ∑fixi/N
      Where N=∑f

    • For Ungrouped data

      • xˉ = ∑fx/∑f

    • Median: Used when data is skewed or contains outliers.
    • For Grouped Data

      • Median = L+(n/2-m/f)h

  • Mode: Used for categorical or most frequent data.

7. Skewness

Skewness measures asymmetry in a distribution.

  • Positive skew: Mean > Median > Mode
  • Negative skew: Mean < Median < Mode
  • Symmetric: Mean = Median = Mode

Coefficient of Skewness

Karl Pearson’s Coefficient of Skewness:

Formula:
Sk = (Mean − Mode) / Standard Deviation

Bowley’s Coefficient of Skewness:

Formula:
Sb = (Q3 + Q1 − 2Q2) / (Q3 − Q1)

Quartiles

Quartiles divide the ordered data into four equal parts.

Quartile formulas (Grouped Data):

Q1 = l + [((N/4) − cf) / f] × h

Q2 (Median) = l + [((N/2) − cf) / f] × h

Q3 = l + [((3N/4) − cf) / f] × h

where:
l = lower class boundary of quartile class
N = total frequency
cf = cumulative frequency before quartile class
f = frequency of quartile class
h = class width

Interquartile Range (IQR)

IQR = Q3 − Q1

8. Coefficient of Variation & Skewness

Coefficient of Variation (CV) measures relative dispersion in data.

Formula:
CV = (Standard Deviation / Mean) × 100

A lower CV indicates greater consistency in the data.

10. Effect of Outliers

Outliers are extreme values that significantly affect the mean and standard deviation but have little effect on the median and quartiles.

11. Effect of Removing Outliers

After removing outliers, the data becomes more representative and reliable. Measures such as mean and standard deviation become more stable.
